Biological Effects of Electromagnetic Fields and Radiation

A Summary of Scientific Findings

Based on the paper by Z. Sienkiewicz, NRPB, UK

Introduction: The State of Research

  • Extensive research on low-intensity EMF exposure exists, mainly using rodents and cell cultures.
  • The literature is diverse and often phenomenological, lacking a coherent strategy.
  • Results are often inconsistent and contradictory, with few replication studies.
  • Conclusion: Very few biological effects have been established, and no adverse health effects have been confirmed.

Part 1: Time-Varying Fields

(Frequencies less than 100 kHz)

How They Work: Induced Currents

  • Exposure induces electric fields and currents within biological tissues.
  • High Intensity: Can stimulate nerves and muscles.
  • Low Intensity: May modulate brain/spinal cord activity.
  • Perceptual Effects:
    • Magnetic Phosphenes: Faint, flickering visual sensations (threshold ~15 mT at 50 Hz).
    • Hair Vibration: Caused by surface charges (perceived at ~3-12 kV/m). Annoying above 15 kV/m.

Physiology & Behaviour

  • Little evidence for consistent effects on heart, lungs, blood, or immune system.
  • Most observed effects are subtle changes in the central nervous system (CNS).
  • Melatonin Hypothesis: Chronic exposure was reported to reduce melatonin in rats, a hormone linked to cancer. However, recent studies show this link may not be robust.
  • No functional impairment has been clearly established from these subtle changes.

Reproduction & Cancer Concerns

  • Reproduction: Despite early studies on chick embryos, most mammalian studies show NO consistent effects on fertility, development, or pregnancy outcome from typical exposures (e.g., VDUs).
  • Cancer Initiation: No convincing evidence that EMFs cause genetic damage (initiation).
  • Cancer Promotion: Experimental evidence is contradictory and does not provide a clear indication of an effect on tumour promotion.

Part 2: Microwave & Radiofrequency Radiation

(Frequencies greater than 100 kHz)

Primary Effect: Tissue Heating

  • The vast majority of biological effects are consistent with responses to induced heating (rises of ~$1^{\circ}C$ or more).
  • Effects show clear thresholds; below these, effects are insignificant for the general population.
  • Sensitive Tissues: Eyes and testes are known to be sensitive to heat.
  • Embryo/Fetus: May be sensitive as heat is an established teratogen.

Pulsed Radiation & Non-Thermal Effects

  • Microwave Hearing: Humans can perceive pulsed RF as a clicking/buzzing noise, caused by thermoelastic expansion of brain tissue.
  • Eye Changes: Degenerative changes reported in animal eyes after protracted exposure to pulsed microwaves, even without significant heating.
  • Non-Thermal Effects: Some controversial effects reported (e.g., changes in calcium ion mobility in brain tissue), but they are not firmly established.

RF Radiation & Cancer Concerns

  • Public concern is increasing with the use of mobile communication devices.
  • Initiation: RF radiation lacks the energy to directly damage DNA. It is not considered a cancer initiator.
  • Promotion: Limited evidence that RF radiation affects cell growth. This question is less clear.
  • Progression: The ability of RF radiation to affect tumour progression is equivocal; no clearly defined effects can be seen.

Overall Summary

  • Low Frequency Fields (<100 kHz): Apart from perception, few consistent effects are found. No persuasive evidence for developmental or carcinogenic effects.
  • RF Radiation (>100 kHz): Most effects are due to heating and are insignificant at typical exposure levels.
  • Cancer: EMF/RF is not considered a cancer initiator. Its role in promotion or progression remains unclear and lacks strong evidence.
  • Bottom Line: The available scientific evidence does not confirm adverse health effects from typical environmental exposure to EMF.
